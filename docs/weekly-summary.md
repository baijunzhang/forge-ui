## Version 1 — with internal team context

**Subject: Weekly Summary – AI Markets Internship**

Hi all,

I wanted to share a brief summary of what I worked on and learned this week.

This week, I focused on understanding how AI Markets can be embedded into different front-office workflows, especially across Sales and Trading desks. I shadowed and spoke with several desks, including **FX Options Trading, Collateral Treasury Trading, Credit Flow Trading, Greater China FX Trading, and Wealth Sales**. These conversations helped me better understand not only what each desk does, but also where AI Markets can create real workflow value.

For **FX Options Trading**, I learned that the desk is highly focused on price making and risk management across G10, EM, CNH, vanilla options, digitals and exotics. Key inputs include implied volatility, smile, gamma, tenor, spot, rates and cross-asset context. The trader currently sees value in AI Markets for market updates and workflow support, but also highlighted that a more useful tool would need deeper integration with pricing and risk data. One specific suggestion was to incorporate pricing APIs so that traders can directly access relative smile and gamma data, rather than manually pulling them from separate systems.

For **Collateral Treasury Trading**, I learned that the desk focuses on funding, repo, stock loans, evergreen repo, collateral optimization, balance sheet usage and liquidity constraints. The key takeaway was that AI Markets could be valuable not only as an information assistant, but as a workflow tool that helps with trade booking, validation checks, collateral information retrieval and basket visibility. A particularly useful direction would be connecting AI Markets with collateral-level APIs, so users can directly see collateral information and constraints instead of searching across multiple systems.

For **Credit Flow Trading**, I learned more about how credit traders think about bonds, credit spreads, coupon, tenor, bid-offer, liquidity, client flow and book risk. The desk highlighted the usefulness of AI Markets in summarizing earnings reports and company guidance from a credit perspective, rather than just an equity perspective. Another potential future direction they mentioned is auto-hedge support, where AI Markets could help suggest hedge options or show the risk impact after a trade, while leaving the final decision to the trader.

For **Greater China FX Trading**, I learned that the desk covers spot, NDFs and FX swaps across Hong Kong, Mainland China and Taiwan, with Taiwan being more equity-related. The workflow is highly flow-driven, and PnL can come from spread, volume and risk-taking. A key pain point they mentioned was counterparty limit monitoring. Before trading, the desk needs to check whether each counterparty still has available limit. One potential AI Markets use case would be a live board showing each counterparty's trading activity and limit usage during the day, automatically flagging counterparties close to or above the bank's limit so the desk can apply for approval in time.

I also shadowed the **Wealth Sales** desk because my assigned project is related to wealth sales. Since the desk sells structured products, I spent time learning the relevant financial product knowledge and how these products are explained, positioned and distributed to clients. This helped me better understand what kind of AI support would be useful in a client-facing sales workflow, such as product explanation, market updates, idea generation and client-ready summaries.

Project-wise, I met with my mentor **Sarang** and discussed how different AI models should be prompted differently depending on their strengths, speed and reasoning capabilities. This was helpful for thinking about how to design prompts and workflows for the Wealth Sales project. I also worked on the **AI Markets Desktop** project with Junxing, where I assisted with UI design and testing. During testing, I shared several function and UX suggestions, including stronger conversation memory, the ability to quote or reference previous answers, clearer progress indicators for long-running questions, and better controls for stopping, pausing or tracking agentic workflows.

Overall, this week helped me connect the dots between business workflows, user needs and product design. One key takeaway is that AI Markets should not be viewed as a generic chatbot, but as a desk-specific workflow copilot. Each desk has different data, products, risks and pain points, so the most valuable AI use cases are the ones that connect directly into the desk's daily workflow. This also helped me better understand what was mentioned previously about experiencing the full workflow of the business, from production to testing. Through testing the product myself, I realized that product testing is not only about checking whether something works, but also about identifying new user needs and potential improvements.

Best,
Lisa

## Version 2 — paragraph form, without internal team conversations

**Subject: Weekly Summary – Desk Shadowing, AI Markets Use Cases and Project Updates**

Hi all,

I wanted to share a summary of what I worked on and learned this week. Overall, this week helped me better understand how AI Markets can support different Markets desks in a more workflow-specific way. Through desk shadowing, project work, and product testing, I learned that the value of AI Markets is not only in generating answers, but also in helping users complete real front-office workflows, such as retrieving pricing or risk data, summarizing market information, checking counterparty limits, supporting product explanation, and improving testing or UI experience.

This week, I shadowed several desks, including FX Options Trading, Collateral Treasury Trading, Credit Flow Trading, Greater China FX Trading, and Wealth Sales. For FX Options Trading, I learned that the desk focuses on price making and risk management across products such as G10, EM, CNH, vanilla options, digitals, and exotics. The trader explained that key inputs include spot, tenor, notional, implied volatility, smile, gamma, rates, and cross-asset market context. One of my main takeaways was that FX Options traders need to translate public information and market data into option pricing, hedging, and book risk decisions. From an AI Markets perspective, the trader saw value in daily or weekly updates, PnL report auto-run and explanation, implied volatility analysis, and more desk-specific risk support. One specific suggestion was to incorporate pricing APIs so that AI Markets can directly retrieve pricing and risk data. The trader also mentioned the need to directly access relative smile and gamma data, especially for understanding basket hedge risk and the impact of hedging on the book. This helped me understand that for FX Options, AI Markets would be most useful if it becomes a pricing and risk workflow copilot rather than a general market summary tool.

For Collateral Treasury Trading, I learned that the desk focuses on funding, repo, stock loans, evergreen repo, collateral optimization, balance sheet usage, liquidity constraints, and cross-currency basis. The discussion helped me understand that this desk is not mainly about taking directional market views, but about using collateral and funding tools to obtain cheaper or more stable funding while managing balance sheet and liquidity constraints. We discussed concepts such as repo using US Treasuries, overnight market funding, evergreen repo, stock loans, cross-currency basis, interest rate parity, and USD funding premium. From an AI Markets perspective, the most relevant use cases were workflow-based. The desk would benefit from tools that can help automate trade booking, extract trade details from emails or instructions, map trade information into the correct fields, support validation checks, and assist ops checks. A particularly important suggestion was that AI Markets could connect to APIs that provide collateral-level information. Instead of searching across systems or only looking at trade-level information, users could directly see collateral details, basket visibility, eligibility, constraints, and related validation information. This helped me understand that for Collateral Treasury Trading, AI Markets could become a collateral and funding workflow copilot.

For Credit Flow Trading, I learned more about how credit traders think about bonds, spread, coupon, tenor, bid-offer, liquidity, client flow, issuer risk, and book risk. The desk is more OTC and flow-driven, so the trader needs to respond to client inquiries, make markets, quote bid-offer, manage inventory, and control spread and issuer exposure. One use case that stood out was using AI Markets to summarize earnings reports and company guidance from a credit perspective. This means focusing not only on equity-style earnings reaction, but also on leverage, cash flow, debt level, liquidity, refinancing risk, rating implications, and possible spread impact. The trader also mentioned auto-hedge as a potential future direction. My understanding is that AI Markets would not make the hedge decision automatically, but could help suggest possible hedge options or show how risk changes after a trade. For example, after taking on a bond position, the tool could help show issuer exposure, spread risk, duration or rates risk, and potential hedge choices. This helped me understand that Credit Flow Trading would benefit from an AI tool that understands issuer information, bond-level data, spread movement, client flow, and hedge or risk impact.

For Greater China FX Trading, I learned that the desk covers FX flow trading across Hong Kong, Mainland China, and Taiwan, with Taiwan being more equity-related. The main products include spot, NDFs, and FX swaps. The desk serves different types of clients and counterparties, including banks, brokers, corporates, hedge funds, and real money clients. The workflow is highly flow-driven, and PnL can come from bid-offer spread, trading volume, and risk-taking from positions. One important point I learned is that the desk needs to understand client behavior and preference pricing: which clients can trade, which clients are profitable, which clients can be charged wider or tighter spreads, and which client flows can help offset the desk's existing risk direction. The clearest AI Markets suggestion from this desk was around counterparty limit automation. Before making trades, the desk needs to check whether each counterparty still has available limit. If a counterparty goes beyond the bank's limit and the desk misses it, it becomes a desk issue, even though approval is usually granted if requested in time. One potential AI Markets function would be a live board showing each counterparty's trading activity and limit usage during the day, automatically flagging counterparties that are close to or above the limit so the desk can apply for approval in time. This was a very concrete example of how AI Markets could reduce manual checking and help manage workflow risk.

I also shadowed the Wealth Sales desk because my assigned project is related to Wealth Sales. Since the desk sells structured products, I spent time learning the relevant financial product knowledge and understanding how these products are explained and positioned in a client-facing sales workflow. This helped me better understand that Wealth Sales requires AI support that is not only technically accurate, but also easy to understand and suitable for client or relationship-manager communication. Based on the shadowing, I think relevant AI Markets use cases could include structured product explanation, client-ready market updates, product idea support, and helping users translate product features and market views into clearer sales language. This was useful for my project because it connected the technical AI workflow with the actual product and sales context.

Project-wise, I met with my mentor Sarang and learned more about how different AI models should be prompted differently. Since my assigned project is related to Wealth Sales, this was helpful for thinking about how to design prompts based on the target user, the product type, the desired output format, and whether the output is for internal understanding or client-facing use. This also helped me realize that prompt design should not be generic. For a Wealth Sales workflow, prompts need to guide the model to produce clear, structured, product-relevant, and user-appropriate outputs.

I also worked on the AI Markets Desktop project with Junxing, where I assisted with UI design and testing. During testing, I shared several function and UX suggestions based on my own user experience. One suggestion was stronger conversation memory, so that AI Markets can remember user-confirmed context within a workflow and reuse it later. Another suggestion was the ability to quote or reference previous answers more easily, so users can continue from a specific part of the conversation without manually copying content. I also suggested adding a progress indicator for each question or long-running task, similar to a chat or agent workflow, so users can see whether the system is reading input, searching, generating, or validating output. In addition, I suggested better control for long-running or agentic workflows, such as showing the current step, avoiding unexplained loops, and allowing users to pause, stop, or track progress more clearly. These suggestions came from testing the product directly and thinking about how users would feel when using AI Markets for real workflows.

**Overall Takeaway**

One of my biggest takeaways this week is that each desk has different data, products, risks, workflows, and pain points, so the most valuable AI use cases are the ones that connect directly into the desk's daily workflow. Through shadowing different desks, I realized that building useful AI tools for Markets requires both AI capability and a strong understanding of business flow. Understanding only the technology is not enough, because the tool needs to solve real business problems; but understanding only the workflow is also not enough, because the value comes from knowing how AI can be embedded into that workflow in a practical and scalable way.

This also helped me better understand what was mentioned previously about experiencing the full workflow of the business, from production to testing. While working on AI Markets Desktop with Junxing, I assisted with UI design and testing, and during the testing process I also shared several function and UX suggestions. This made me realize that product testing is not only about checking whether something works, but also about experiencing the product from the user's perspective, identifying friction points, and discovering new user needs or potential improvements. Through this process, I felt that testing itself can also become an important source of product ideas, because when I use the tool myself, I can better understand how real users may interact with it and where AI Markets can further improve.

Best,
Lisa
